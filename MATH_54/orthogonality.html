1. Orthogonality

Two vectors \(u\) and \(v\) are called orthogonal when \(u \cdot v = 0\), or when the norm (magnitude) of the two vectors satisfies the famous rule of Pythagoras: \(\left | u \right |^2 + \left | v \right |^2 = \left | u + v \right |^2\).

More broadly, we can say that if \(v \in \mathbb{R}^n\), and \(W\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^n\), that \(v\) is orthogonal to \(W\) if it is orthogonal to every vector in \(W\). We call \(W^{\perp}\) the orthogonal complement of \(W\), consisting of all the vectors orthogonal to \(W\).

An important application of orthogonality comes in the form of vector spaces. We can say that the row space of \(A\) is orthogonal to the nullspace of \(A\):

\begin{align} (\text{Row }A)^{\perp} = \text{Nul }A \end{align}

This is because by the definition of the nullspace, vectors in the nullspace, when multiplied by vectors in the row space, yield the zero vector. Similarly:

\begin{align} (\text{Col }A)^{\perp}= \text{Nul } (A^T) \end{align}
